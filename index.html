I am a research coordinator at the Stanford Social Neuroscience Lab, mentored by Prof. Jamil Zaki and Dr. Rui Pei, working on the Stanford Communities Project. When I am not full-time-researching at SSNL, I am also collaborating with Prof. Diyi Yang and Caleb Ziems at the SALT Lab in Stanford CS and NLP Group.

Previously, I received my B.S. in Data Science (with Honors) and B.A. in Psychology (with Honors) from The University of Chicago, advised by Prof. Yuan Chang Leong and worked with Ren Calabro at the Computational Affective and Social Neuroscience Laboratory and the Data Science Institute.

I am broadly interested in how social technologies shape human behavior and social cognition, mainly social norms, biases, and group/collective processes. I am particularly interested in how communicative technologies, such as language systems, online and offline media, (and now) chatbots, and large language models, mediate social influence and refine how people seek, share, and interpret information.

  • How do emerging digital technologies shape the ways we form beliefs about the world, identify and coordinate norms, and learn about social structures?
  • How do digital environments navigate how we understand social systems?
  • What does happen when we seek, share, and interpret ultimately social information about the world around us with access to global environments (as opposed to local communities) defined by algorithmically mediated, infinite information sources?

My past work covers how we learn stereotypes and social norms from spoken language, diffusion of information (norms, affects, and biases) within online and offline social networks, and divergent interpretations of narratives we consume (news, books, media...).

Conference Page   Poster   Abstract

Young adults in the U.S. report rising political anxiety and declining wellbeing, yet little is known about how they navigate everyday social life under major collective stressors. To investigate, we surveyed ~3,000 undergraduates from the same community in a three-week ecological momentary assessment (EMA) study spanning the 2024 U.S. national election, along with pre- and post-election trait surveys six months apart. Participants completed daily EMAs on wellbeing (happiness, sadness, stress, anger) and social interactions (partner, closeness, content, quality). We use multilevel models to test two hypotheses: Selectionâ€“whether reactivity to election stress (anticipatory and recovery) predicted shifts in interactions (e.g., avoiding extremity or high-disagreement, seeking homophilous or norm-consistent ties); and Bufferingâ€“whether interactions moderate stress-affect links. Preliminary findings suggest that while having an interaction generally buffers stress, interactions between certain groups, such as low-income, high-stress students, can predict higher stress and lower wellbeing. Our results reveal how collective stressors ripple through young adult social networks.
