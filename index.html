I am broadly interested in how social technologies, social norms, and group/collective processes shape human behavior and social cognition. I am particularly interested in how communicative technologies, such as language systems, online and offline media, (and now) chatbots, and large language models, mediate social influence and shape how people seek, share, and interpret information.

My past work covers anything from how we learn stereotypes and social norms from spoken language to social networks analysis and building socially-aware LLMs.


about me

I am a research coordinator at Stanford University, mentored by Prof. Jamil Zaki and Dr. Rui Pei at the Stanford Social Neuroscience Lab. I am mainly working on the Stanford Communities Project, which is a community-based (~20k students), long-term (~8 years) study that aims to understand young-adult social networks, social norms, group behaviors, well-being, and personality traits through trait, behavior, and ecological momenraty assesment (EMA) surveys.

When I am not full-time-researching at SSNL, I am also collaborating with Prof. Diyi Yang and Caleb Ziems at the SALT Lab at Stanford HAI and NLP, learning more about how language technologies mediate social cognition, cultures, and collective beliefs, as well as what it means to make them "socially-aware" or "human-centered."

Previously, I received my B.S. in Data Science (with Honors) and B.A. in Psychology (with Honors) from The University of Chicago. I was fortunate to be advised by Prof. Yuan Chang Leong and work with Ren Calabro at the Computational Affective and Social Neuroscience Laboratory and the Data Science Institute. We were using a combination of methods from neuroscience (fMRI), linguistics (NLP), and computer science (LLMs), to refine existing approaches for measuring political bias in online media and people.
